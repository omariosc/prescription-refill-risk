How It Works

From raw prescription records to an actionable risk score — a six-stage pipeline built for transparency.

1

database Data Collection (CMS DE-SynPUF)

We start with eight linked datasets covering three years of anonymised prescription records, hospital admissions, outpatient visits, and physician encounters — joined together for each patient.

description 5.5 million prescription fills groups 99,538 patients calendar_month 2008–2010
2

cleaning_services Cleaning & Validation

Records with missing supply durations or cancelled prescriptions are removed. Clinical codes are standardised, date formats are unified, and patients who passed away are appropriately handled so their data doesn't distort the model.

filter_alt 117,749 invalid records removed sync_alt 11 condition flags remapped person_off 2,301 death-censored fills
3

label Defining “Late” (Label Construction)

For each prescription, we calculate when the supply should run out. If the patient's next refill arrives more than seven days after that date, we mark it as late. This gives us a clear yes-or-no target the model can learn from.

assignment 1.47 million labelled fills timer 7-day grace window
4

construction Building the Signals (Feature Engineering)

We extract 67 measurable signals from each patient's history — including how regular their past refills have been, their age, chronic conditions, hospital visits, and prescription costs. Each signal only uses information available before the prediction point, preventing the model from “seeing the future”.

5

model_training Training the Model (LightGBM)

A gradient-boosted decision tree model learns which combinations of signals best predict late refills. Crucially, training uses only historical data and is tested on a separate future time period — just as it would work in practice.

6

tune Calibration & Uncertainty

Raw model scores are mapped to real-world risk tiers based on actual observed late rates in our test population. Each prediction also includes a confidence range — when the model is unsure, it says so, so clinicians can apply their own judgement.
